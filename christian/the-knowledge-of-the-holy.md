# The Knowledge of the Holy

## Author Background

Aiden Wilson Tozer (April 21, 1897 – May 12, 1963) was an American Christian pastor, author, magazine editor, and spiritual mentor. Born into poverty in the tiny farming community of La Jose, Pennsylvania, Tozer was largely self-educated. He came to Christianity as a teenager in Akron, Ohio, after overhearing a street preacher share the simple gospel message. Without formal theological education, he began his ministry in 1919 and spent 44 years associated with the Christian and Missionary Alliance denomination, serving for 30 years as pastor of Southside Alliance Church in Chicago (1928-1959), and later at Avenue Road Church in Toronto until his death in 1963.

Tozer was known for his prophetic voice against worldly compromise in the church, his emphasis on intimate knowledge of God, and his simple, non-materialistic lifestyle—he never owned a car even after becoming a well-known author. He and his wife Ada Cecelia Pfautz had seven children. He received honorary doctorates from Wheaton and Houghton colleges.

## Book Summary

Published in 1961, *The Knowledge of the Holy* stands as one of the most impactful works in modern Christian literature on the doctrine of God. A.W. Tozer wrote this book out of deep concern that the contemporary church had lost its reverence for and understanding of who God truly is. The book serves as both a theological treatise and a spiritual awakening, calling believers to recognize the majesty, holiness, and incomprehensibility of God.

Tozer's central thesis is that "the most important thing about a man is what he thinks about God." He argues that right thinking about God's attributes leads to right living, and that a diminished view of God produces a diminished Christian life. The book is not merely academic; it is deeply devotional, designed to stir the heart as well as inform the mind.

Each chapter explores a different attribute of God, moving from foundational concepts like God's self-existence and infinity to more complex theological themes. Tozer's writing style is poetic yet accessible, combining Scripture, hymnody, and personal reflection. He draws heavily on the great puritan and Reformed theologians while maintaining a mystical sensitivity that appeals to readers across denominational lines.

The book challenges the casual familiarity that characterizes much modern Christianity, urging readers to recover a sense of holy fear and wonder in their approach to God. Tozer contends that without an elevated view of God, prayer becomes mechanical, worship becomes routine, and the Christian life loses its transformative power.

## Chapter-by-Chapter Summary

1. **The Attributes of God** – Introduction emphasizing that what we think about God determines the quality of our spiritual lives; calls for a return to high and holy thinking about God.

2. **The Self-existence of God** – Explores God's eternal, uncaused existence apart from and prior to creation; He is the "I AM" who depends on nothing beyond Himself.

3. **The Infinity of God** – God's boundlessness in space and time; He is without limits yet actively involved in His creation.

4. **The Omnipotence of God** – God's unlimited power; He speaks and worlds come into being; His power is exercised in perfect wisdom and love.

5. **The Omniscience of God** – God's all-knowing nature; He knows the end from the beginning and searches the hearts of men.

6. **The Omnipresence of God** – God's presence everywhere at once; He is nearer than our thoughts and present in every circumstance.

7. **The Immutability of God** – God's unchanging nature; unlike His creation, God does not change in His being, perfections, or purposes.

8. **The Wisdom of God** – God's perfect knowledge and planning; His purposes are accomplished through means that display infinite intelligence.

9. **The Faithfulness of God** – God's reliability and trustworthiness; He never fails to fulfill His promises.

10. **The Goodness of God** – God's moral excellence and benevolent nature; His goodness is the foundation of His law and grace.

11. **The Mercy of God's Love** – The intersection of God's love and justice; mercy is available because of Christ's atoning work.

12. **The Grace of God** – Unmerited favor toward sinners; grace is more than forgiveness—it is transforming power.

13. **The Holiness of God** – God's set-apart, otherness; true holiness is found only in God, and He demands it of His people.

14. **The Sovereignty of God** – God's absolute authority and right to rule over all His creation.

15. **The Wrath of God** – God's righteous anger against sin; His wrath is the necessary counterpart to His holiness and love.

16. **The Love of God** – The highest attribute; God's love is eternal, self-giving, and the basis for all redemption.

## Theological Analysis

*The Knowledge of the Holy* represents a significant contribution to evangelical theology, particularly in its emphasis on the nature and attributes of God. Tozer's theological approach is broadly Reformed, affirming doctrines such as God's self-existence (aseity), immutability, omnipotence, omniscience, and omnipresence. However, he presents these classical doctrines in an accessible, devotional manner that transcends narrow theological categories.

The book's theological strength lies in its insistence that correct doctrine leads to spiritual vitality. Tozer famously wrote, "We may as well face the fact that the whole thing rests upon the kind of God we believe in." This approach, sometimes called "theological pragmatism," holds that orthodox belief about God produces holy living.

Tozer's treatment of each attribute combines biblical fidelity with practical application. His discussion of God's immutability addresses modern concerns about process theology and religious pluralism. His exploration of God's love and wrath avoids both sentimentalism and antinomianism, presenting a balanced vision of a God who is both tender and terrible.

The book also reflects Tozer's concern for spiritual formation. He believed that contemporary Christianity had traded the "awesome dread" of God for a comfortable familiarity that robbed the faith of its transforming power. His theology is doxological—it aims not merely to inform but to produce worship.

## Key Takeaways

1. **Right thinking about God is foundational** – The quality of our spiritual life is directly proportional to our understanding of who God is.

2. **God's attributes are not isolated doctrines** – They form a coherent whole that reveals His character and invites our worship.

3. **Intellectual knowledge is insufficient** – True knowledge of God is experiential and transformative, affecting how we pray, worship, and live.

4. **A high view of God produces holy living** – When we truly grasp God's holiness, we are compelled to pursue holiness ourselves.

5. **God's attributes should produce wonder** – The doctrines of God's infinity, omnipotence, and omniscience should fill us with awe and reverence.

6. **God's love must be balanced with His holiness and wrath** – A truly biblical understanding of God includes both His tender mercy and His righteous anger against sin.

7. **Christian maturity requires growing knowledge of God** – The Christian life is a journey into deeper knowledge of the One who is infinitely knowable yet ultimately incomprehensible.

## Overall Assessment

*The Knowledge of the Holy* remains a landmark work in Christian literature, celebrated for its eloquent prose, theological depth, and spiritual passion. Tozer's ability to communicate complex theological concepts with clarity and beauty makes this book accessible to new believers and scholars alike.

The book's enduring strength is its consistent focus on the practical implications of doctrine. Tozer never allows theology to become mere speculation; every attribute of God is presented as a call to worship and obedience. This approach has made the book a staple in Christian education and spiritual formation curricula for decades.

Critics might note that the book is more inspirational than exhaustive—Tozer does not engage with alternative theological perspectives or offer extensive biblical exposition for each attribute. However, this simplicity is part of the book's charm and accessibility. It serves as an excellent introduction to systematic theology while remaining rich enough for repeated study.

The book's influence is evident in its continued publication and widespread recommendation across evangelical churches. It has shaped generations of believers in their understanding of God and continues to challenge contemporary Christianity to recover a proper sense of reverence and awe in worship.

For anyone seeking to deepen their understanding of God and revitalize their spiritual life, *The Knowledge of the Holy* remains an essential read—a book that not only informs the mind but transforms the heart.
