# The Pursuit of God

**Author:** A.W. Tozer  
**Publication Date:** 1948  
**Category:** Christian Spirituality / Spiritual Formation

---

## Author Background

Aiden Wilson Tozer (April 21, 1897 – May 12, 1963) was an American Christian pastor, author, magazine editor, and spiritual mentor. Born into poverty in the farming community of La Jose, Pennsylvania, Tozer was largely self-educated, having taught himself what he missed in high school and university. Converted to Christianity as a teenager in Akron, Ohio, Tozer accepted his first pastoral position in 1919 without any formal training in Christian theology.

For 44 years, Tozer ministered with the Christian and Missionary Alliance, serving 33 of those years as pastor of Southside Alliance Church in Chicago (1928-1959), and later at Avenue Road Church in Toronto until his death in 1963. Known for his prophetic voice and emphasis on holiness, worship, and the deeper spiritual life, Tozer was deeply concerned about the church's tendency toward compromise with worldly concerns. He never owned a car, preferring simple travel by bus and train, and often gave away his royalties to those in need.

## TLDR Summary

*The Pursuit of God* is a passionate call for Christians to pursue a deeper, more intimate relationship with God. Written in poetic, prophetic style, Tozer challenges believers to move beyond superficial faith and experience the transformative presence of God in their daily lives. The book emphasizes that God desires to be known personally and intimately, not merely acknowledged intellectually.

## Book Summary

Published in 1948, *The Pursuit of God* emerged from Tozer's deep concern that modern Christianity had become comfortable and complacent, settling for a "表面的宗教" (surface religion) rather than genuine communion with the living God. The book reads less like a systematic theology and more like a prophetic call to awakening—a passionate plea for believers to recognize that God is intimately present and actively seeking relationship with His creation.

Tozer writes from personal experience and deep reflection, sharing his own journey of spiritual awakening while challenging readers to examine their hearts. He argues that the Christian life was never meant to be mediocre or routine, but rather a dynamic pursuit of God's presence. The book's eight chapters guide readers through various aspects of this pursuit: from recognizing God's speaking voice, to understanding the role of the Holy Spirit, to experiencing the reality of God's presence in everyday life.

The book's central thesis is captured in its opening words: "God is a Person, and in the deep center of our being it is His will that we should find Him." Tozer contends that too many Christians have settled for a "borrowed faith" — the beliefs and experiences of others — rather than pursuing their own genuine encounter with God. This pursuit, he insists, requires surrender, humility, and a willingness to let go of anything that competes for our affection.

## Chapter-by-Chapter Summary

### Chapter 1: "The Weight of Glory"
Tozer introduces the book's central theme: the Christian's deep longing for God's presence. He contrasts the superficial faith of his day with the passionate pursuit of God seen in biblical characters. Tozer argues that God has placed eternity in human hearts, creating a void that only He can fill. He warns against the danger of being satisfied with religious activity rather than genuine communion with God.

### Chapter 2: "The Speaking Voice"
This chapter explores how God communicates with His children. Tozer emphasizes that God still speaks today and that the Holy Scripture serves as the primary means through which God reveals Himself. He cautions against quenching the Spirit's voice and encourages believers to develop sensitive hearts that can recognize God's leading.

### Chapter 3: "The Foreignness of the Spiritual"
Tozer addresses the tension between the natural (fleshly) and spiritual realms. He explains that spiritual things appear "foolish" to the unregenerate mind because they are foreign to human nature. The Christian must learn to live in the spiritual dimension, which requires dying to self and being reborn in the Spirit.

### Chapter 4: "The Divine Conquest"
The focus shifts to how God accomplishes His work in the believer's heart. Tozer discusses the Holy Spirit's role in conviction, conversion, and sanctification. He emphasizes that God must do a "strange work" in breaking down the old self before He can build up the new.

### Chapter 5: " "Spiritual Lawyering""
This chapter examines the danger of treating faith as a legal transaction rather than a living relationship. Tozer critiques Christians who approach God as if He were a cosmic judge to be appeased rather than a Father to be loved. True faith, he argues, involves the whole person—heart, soul, mind, and strength.

### Chapter 6: "The Holy Spirit"
Tozer provides a thoughtful exploration of the Holy Spirit's person and work. He emphasizes the Spirit's deity, personality, and essential role in the Christian life. The chapter serves as a corrective to both charismatic excess and spiritual dryness, presenting a balanced view of the Spirit's work in empowering believers for worship and service.

### Chapter 7: "The Sacrifice of the Intellect"
In this provocative chapter, Tozer challenges the modern tendency to reduce Christianity to a set of propositional truths. While affirming the importance of sound doctrine, he insists that intellectual assent alone is insufficient. True knowledge of God comes through personal experience, not merely correct thinking.

### Chapter 8: "The Missing Jewel"
The final chapter identifies what is missing in much of modern Christianity: the conscious, continual presence of God. Tozer calls for a return to the "hidden life" — the intimate, personal pursuit of God's face that characterized the deepest saints throughout history. He closes with an urgent appeal for readers to begin this pursuit immediately.

## Theological Analysis

*The Pursuit of God* represents a distinctively evangelical mystical tradition that emphasizes personal encounter with God while maintaining strong biblical foundations. Tozer's theology is deeply trinitarian, with particular attention to the Father's initiative in seeking us, the Son's redemption as the means of reconciliation, and the Holy Spirit's ongoing work in making God real to believers.

The book reflects Tozer's commitment to the authority of Scripture while critiquing a faith that remains merely intellectual. His emphasis on the immanence of God—God's active presence with and within believers—balances the sometimes abstract theological formulations of systematic theology. At the same time, his focus on experience has sometimes drawn criticism from more academically-minded theologians who worry about subjectivism.

Tozer's soteriology emphasizes the transformative nature of salvation, viewing conversion not merely as legal declaration but as the beginning of a lifelong journey toward Christlikeness. His understanding of sanctification is notably Wesleyan in its emphasis on progressive holiness, though he avoids rigid systematic categories.

Perhaps most distinctive is Tozer's prophetic critique of American evangelicalism's tendency toward comfort, success, and worldliness. He consistently challenged believers to examine whether their faith was producing genuine godliness or merely religious activity that left the heart unchanged.

## Key Takeaways

1. **God is a Person Who Seeks Intimacy** – The central message of the book is that God desires personal relationship with each believer, not mere religious observance or intellectual assent.

2. **True Faith Requires Pursuit** – Christianity is not a passive religion but an active pursuit of God's presence. Believers must "press on" toward deeper relationship with Him.

3. **The Holy Spirit Makes God Real** – The Holy Spirit is essential in revealing Christ to us and making the things of God real and personal in our experience.

4. **Superficial Faith Is Insufficient** – Going through religious motions without genuine transformation is dangerous. God wants our hearts, not just our lip service.

5. **The Cross Must Be Embraced** – Following Christ requires dying to self, surrendering our ambitions, and embracing the path of humility and obedience.

6. **Worship Is the Christian's Primary Duty** – True worship is not confined to church services but encompasses the entire Christian life lived in awareness of God's presence.

7. **Biblical Knowledge Alone Is Incomplete** – While doctrine matters, intellectual knowledge of God without heart experience produces pride rather than godliness.

8. **Simplicity and Humility Are Essential** – Tozer models and advocates for a simple, non-materialistic lifestyle that prioritizes spiritual things over worldly success.

## Overall Assessment

*The Pursuit of God* stands as a timeless classic of Christian spirituality that has influenced millions of believers worldwide. Its poetic, prophetic style distinguishes it from typical devotional literature, offering instead something closer to spiritual prophecy—a ringing call to authentic faith in an age of religious complacency.

Tozer's strengths include his ability to diagnose the spiritual diseases of his era with uncanny accuracy—a diagnosis that remains remarkably relevant today. His emphasis on the personal, experiential knowledge of God provides a necessary corrective to a Christianity that has become overly focused on doctrine without life transformation.

The book's weaknesses, if any, lie in its occasional tendency toward mystical language that could be misunderstood as anti-intellectual. However, Tozer's own deep knowledge of Scripture and orthodox theology makes such concerns largely unfounded. His critique of "mere intellectualism" is not a rejection of theology but a call to let theology become lived reality.

The enduring appeal of *The Pursuit of God* lies in its universal message: that God is near, that He speaks, and that He invites every believer into deeper communion with Himself. In an age of distraction and superficiality, Tozer's call to pursue God with our whole hearts remains as urgent as it was when first written in 1948.

This book is essential reading for any Christian seeking to move beyond religious routine into the vibrant, transformative relationship with God for which we were created.
