# The Training of the Twelve

## Author Background

Alexander Balmain Bruce (1831-1899) was a renowned Scottish theologian and New Testament scholar. He served as Professor of New Testament Exegesis at the Free Church College in Glasgow. Bruce was known for his rigorous scholarship and practical approach to biblical interpretation. He wrote several significant works on the life of Christ and the teachings of the New Testament.

## TLDR Summary

"The Training of the Twelve" examines how Jesus systematically trained His twelve disciples over three years, demonstrating that effective disciple-making requires intentional investment, progressive responsibility, and relational depth. Bruce analyzes the methods, principles, and content of Christ's training program.

## Book Summary

Alexander Balmain Bruce's "The Training of the Twelve" is a scholarly examination of Jesus' disciple-making methodology as observed in the Gospel accounts. Published in 1877, this work remains a foundational text for understanding how Christ developed His followers into leaders capable of continuing His mission.

Bruce argues that the training of the Twelve was comprehensive - encompassing their intellectual, spiritual, and practical development. Jesus used a combination of teaching, modeling, delegation, and progressive responsibility to prepare His disciples for ministry after His departure.

The book explores the unique relationship between Jesus and His disciples, examining how He dealt with their weaknesses, corrected their misunderstandings, and gradually equipped them for leadership. Bruce emphasizes that the disciples were not passive recipients but active learners who often struggled to grasp Christ's teachings.

6. **The Inner Circle** - Special training for Peter, James, and John
7. **The Sending Forth** - Progressive delegation of authority
8. **The Failure and Restoration** - Handling the disciples' failures
9. **The Upper Room Discourse** - Final teachings before the crucifixion
10. **The Post-Resurrection Training** - Preparation for the Spirit-filled mission

## Theological Analysis

Bruce's work reflects a conservative evangelical approach to Scripture, treating the Gospel accounts as reliable historical records. He emphasizes the practical theology of disciple-making, showing how Jesus' methods have direct application to church leadership and Christian education.

The book demonstrates that authentic disciple-making is relational, not programmatic. Jesus invested Himself fully in the lives of His disciples, sharing experiences, meals, prayers, and ministry responsibilities with them.

## Key Takeaways

- Disciple-making requires intentional, personal investment over time
- Progressive delegation helps believers grow in confidence and competence
- Failures in disciples should be met with patience and restoration, not abandonment
- The inner circle of three (Peter, James, John) received intensified training
- Jesus combined teaching (doctrine) with practical experience (praxis)
- Prayer was integral to the training program
- The goal was multiplication - each disciple making other disciples

## Key Quotes

> "Christ's method of training was essentially practical - He made disciples by involving them in ministry."

> "The best training for service is service itself, under proper guidance."

## Overall Assessment

"The Training of the Twelve" remains a valuable resource for understanding biblical disciple-making. Bruce's scholarly yet practical approach provides both historical insight and contemporary application. The book is essential reading for pastors, church leaders, and anyone serious about making disciples.

While written in the 19th century, its principles are timeless and remain highly relevant for modern church leadership and disciple-making strategies.
