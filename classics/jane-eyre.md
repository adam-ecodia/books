# Jane Eyre

**Author:** Charlotte Brontë  
**First Published:** October 19, 1847  
**Genre:** Gothic, Bildungsroman, Romance  
**Category:** Classics

---

## Author Background

Charlotte Brontë (1816-1855) was an English novelist and poet, the third of six children born to Patrick Brontë, an Irish clergyman, and Maria Branwell. Born in Thornton, Yorkshire, Charlotte and her siblings—Emily, Anne, and brother Branwell—would become the famous Brontë family of writers. Maria died when Charlotte was only five years old, and Charlotte and her sisters were sent to the Clergy Daughters' School at Cowan Bridge, where deplorable conditions led to typhus outbreaks that killed Charlotte's two elder sisters. These traumatic experiences informed the harsh Lowood School in Jane Eyre.

After educating her sisters at home, Charlotte became a teacher and later attended a boarding school in Brussels, where she fell in love with a married professor who inspired her character of Rochester. Upon returning to England, the sisters attempted to establish a school (which failed) and published a collection of poetry under male pseudonyms (Currer, Ellis, and Acton Bell). Jane Eyre was her breakthrough novel, published under the pseudonym Currer Bell in 1847. Charlotte was the only one of the siblings to marry, wedding her father's curate Arthur Bell Nicholls in 1854. She died pregnant in 1855, likely from complications.

## Publication Details

Jane Eyre was first published on October 19, 1847, by Smith, Elder & Co. of London, under the pen name "Currer Bell." The first American edition followed in January 1848, published by Harper & Brothers of New York. The novel was originally published in three volumes (the standard Victorian format), consisting of 38 chapters. The second edition was dedicated to William Makepeace Thackeray. It was originally subtitled "An Autobiography."

## Sales Information

Jane Eyre has sold over 2 million copies worldwide, making it one of the most successful Victorian novels. It was an immediate success upon publication, going through multiple editions. The novel has never been out of print and continues to sell strongly today, with millions of copies in circulation. It was ranked as the tenth best-loved book in Britain by the BBC's "The Big Read" poll in 2003.

## TLDR Summary

Jane Eyre is a coming-of-age story following its eponymous heroine from childhood through adulthood. Orphaned young, Jane endures a cruel childhood with her aunt and cousins, followed by a harsh education at a charity school. She eventually becomes a governess at Thornfield Hall, where she falls in love with the mysterious Mr. Rochester. After discovering a secret about his past, Jane leaves Thornfield but eventually returns to find Rochester changed by tragedy. The novel explores themes of independence, morality, religion, and social class while championing a heroine who insists on dignity and self-respect.

## Book Summary

Jane Eyre follows the journey of its namesake protagonist from childhood to maturity, presenting one of literature's most compelling portraits of female independence and moral integrity. The novel is narrated in the first person, allowing readers into the innermost thoughts and feelings of Jane as she navigates the challenges of Victorian society.

Jane begins her life at Gateshead Hall with her aunt, Mrs. Reed, and cousins after being orphaned as an infant. Subjected to emotional and physical abuse, Jane develops a fierce, independent spirit. A traumatic event—being locked in the "red room" where her uncle died—sets the tone for her difficult childhood. When Jane is sent to Lowood School, she faces further hardship but also finds friendship with Helen Burns and support from Mr. Brocklehurst's kindlier counterpart, Miss Temple. The conditions at Lowood, based on Charlotte's own experiences at Cowan Bridge, expose the cruelty of Victorian charitable institutions.

Upon completing her education, Jane becomes a governess at Thornfield Hall, where she educates Adèle Varens, a young French girl. It is here that she meets Edward Fairfax Rochester, the brooding master of Thornfield. Their relationship develops through intellectual sparring and growing mutual respect, culminating in Jane's declaration: "I am no bird; and no net ensnares me: I am a free human being with an independent will." However, on the eve of their wedding, Jane discovers that Rochester is already married—to Bertha Mason, a mentally ill woman hidden in the attic. Devastated by this deception, Jane leaves Thornfield with nothing.

Jane wanders homeless on the moors until she is taken in by the Rivers siblings—St. John, Diana, and Mary—who are secretly her cousins. St. John, a clergyman, proposes marriage to Jane, asking her to accompany him to India as his wife. Though tempted by his noble cause, Jane follows her heart and returns to Thornfield, only to find it destroyed by fire (set by Bertha, who dies). Rochester, now blind and widowed, lives at Ferndean. Jane marries him, and they live happily together, eventually having a son who inherits Rochester's sight.

## Plot Outline

**Part 1: Childhood at Gateshead**
- Jane Eyre, orphaned as a baby, lives with her aunt Mrs. Reed at Gateshead Hall
- She is bullied by her cousins, especially John Reed
- As punishment for defending herself, Jane is locked in the red room, where she faints from terror
- The apothecary Mr. Lloyd recommends Jane be sent to school

**Part 2: Education at Lowood**
- Jane enrolls at Lowood Institution, a charity school for girls
- She befriends Helen Burns, an older student with a philosophical outlook
- Helen dies of consumption during a typhus outbreak
- Jane excels academically and eventually becomes a teacher at Lowood

**Part 3: Governess at Thornfield**
- Jane becomes governess to Adèle Varens at Thornfield Hall
- She meets Mr. Rochester, the mysterious master of the house
- Their relationship deepens through conversations and growing mutual attraction
- Strange events occur: a fire in Rochester's room and an attack on Mr. Mason

**Part 4: The Wedding and Discovery**
- Jane and Rochester nearly marry, but a man interrupts to reveal Rochester is already married
- Bertha Mason, Rochester's insane wife, is revealed hidden in the attic
- Jane leaves Thornfield heartbreak

**Part 5: Wandering and the Rivers Family**
- Jane wanders the moors, nearly dying of starvation
- She is taken in by the Rivers family at Moor House
- Jane discovers she is actually their cousin, inheriting money from her uncle
- St. John Rivers proposes, asking Jane to marry him and go to India

**Part 6: Return to Rochester**
- Jane hears Rochester's voice calling to her in the night
- She returns to Thornfield to find it destroyed by fire
- Bertha died in the fire; Rochester is now blind
- Jane marries Rochester; they have a happy life together

## Literary Analysis

### Gothic Elements

Jane Eyre employs numerous Gothic conventions that create atmosphere and heighten drama. Thornfield Hall itself functions as a Gothic setting—a dark, mysterious manor with secrets lurking in its upper reaches. The madwoman in the attic is a quintessential Gothic trope, while the fires, mysterious sounds, and the imposing presence of Rochester all contribute to the genre's conventions. Charlotte Brontë uses these elements not merely for atmosphere but to externalize Jane's psychological state and the secrets that threaten her happiness.

### Bildungsroman Structure

As a bildungsroman, or coming-of-age novel, Jane Eyre traces its protagonist's moral, emotional, and intellectual development from childhood to adulthood. Each stage—Gateshead, Lowood, Thornfield, the moor, and finally Ferndean—represents a phase of growth. At each stage, Jane must assert her independence and maintain her integrity against forces that would diminish her. The novel's structure emphasizes that true maturity comes through experience, suffering, and self-reflection.

### Feminist Themes

Jane Eyre is often cited as an early feminist text. Jane refuses to be subordinate: she demands to be treated with respect, refuses to become Rochester's mistress, and chooses moral integrity over material security. Her famous declaration—"I am no bird; and no net ensnares me"—articulates a vision of female autonomy remarkable for its time. Yet the novel is not strident; Jane's faith and moral seriousness balance her assertiveness, making her a complex rather than didactic heroine.

### The Role of Religion

Religion pervades the novel through multiple characters and interpretations. Helen Burns exemplifies a passive, forgiving Christianity that Jane initially admires but cannot fully embrace. Mr. Brocklehurst represents religious hypocrisy, while St. John Rivers embodies a cold, duty-driven faith that demands the suppression of emotion. Jane ultimately finds a middle path—a personal, sincere faith that integrates her moral independence rather than contradicting it.

### Social Class and Inequality

Throughout the novel, Brontë examines class relations in Victorian England. As a governess, Jane occupies an ambiguous social position—neither servant nor family member. She is acutely aware of the inequalities between herself and Rochester, yet she insists on moral equality. The novel also critiques the treatment of servants and the poor, with Lowood exposing the cruelty that could exist in charitable institutions.

## Key Takeaways

1. **Self-respect and independence are non-negotiable** - Jane would rather suffer than compromise her dignity or become Rochester's mistress

2. **True love requires honesty** - The secret of Bertha Mason destroys Jane and Rochester's relationship; only after transparency can they truly unite

3. **Religious faith and personal autonomy can coexist** - Jane's Christianity is sincere but does not require her to accept injustice or suppress her reason

4. **The past shapes but does not determine the future** - Despite childhood trauma, Jane creates her own destiny through her choices

5. **Economic independence enables personal freedom** - Jane's inheritance gives her the security to return to Rochester on equal footing

6. **Passion must be tempered with principle** - Jane's love for Rochester is real, but she will not abandon her moral compass

## Overall Assessment

Jane Eyre stands as one of the greatest novels in the English language, remarkable for its psychological depth, narrative voice, and pioneering portrayal of female autonomy. Charlotte Brontë created a heroine who would influence generations of writers—Jane's voice, with its combination of passion, morality, and self-knowledge, remains vital and compelling over 175 years later.

The novel succeeds on multiple levels: as a compelling romance, a Gothic thriller, a critique of Victorian society, and a spiritual journey. Brontë's prose, while sometimes criticized for its intensity, perfectly captures Jane's inner life—the tensions between reason and feeling, duty and desire, independence and connection.

Some readers may find the novel's moral framework heavy-handed or its plot convenient (the coincidences that unite the family and restore Jane to Rochester require some suspension of disbelief). However, these are minor criticisms of a work that fundamentally changed the novelistic portrayal of women's interior lives.

Jane Eyre remains essential reading—powerful, moving, and surprisingly modern in its championing of women's right to self-determination and moral integrity.
