# Enchiridion of Epictetus

A Stoic Manual for Living (c. 108 CE)

---

## Author Background

Epictetus (pronounced Epic-TEE-tus) was born in approximately 55 CE in Hierapolis, Phrygia (modern-day Pamukkale, Turkey). Originally a slave in Rome owned by Epaphroditus, a powerful freedman of Emperor Nero, Epictetus endured severe torture that left him lame for life. After gaining his freedom, he studied under the Stoic teacher Musonius Rufus. In 89 CE, following Emperor Domitian's banishment of philosophers from Rome, Epictetus relocated to Nicopolis in Epirus (northwestern Greece), where he established his own influential school.

Despite his physical disability and former slave status, Epictetus attracted many upper-class Roman students, including the notable historian Flavius Arrian (c. 86–160 CE), who preserved his teachings. Ancient sources report that Epictetus was more popular in his day than Plato, and teachers like Herodes Atticus considered him "the greatest of Stoics." He never married and lived to an old age, remaining committed to his philosophy until death around 135 CE.

## Author's Other Works

- **Discourses of Epictetus** (8 books, only 4 survive) — Extensive dialogues recorded by Arrian covering Stoic ethics, logic, and physics
- **The Enchiridion** (Handbook) — This brief manual, also compiled by Arrian, is the subject of this summary
- Various fragments and sayings preserved in later compilations

---

## Publication Details

The Enchiridion (Greek: Ἐγχειρίδιον, meaning "in the hand" or "handbook") was compiled by Arrian around 108 CE, though some scholars place it closer to 125 CE. The work was originally written in Koine Greek and represents Arrian's selection of the most practical and essential teachings from Epictetus's Discourses.

**Publication History:**
- Original Greek compiled: c. 108 CE
- First Latin translation: Niccolò Perotti, 1450
- First printed edition (editio princeps): Angelo Poliziano's Latin translation, 1497
- First Greek publication: 1528 (with Simplicius's commentary)
- Major scholarly edition: Johann Schweighäuser, 1798

## TLDR Summary

The Enchiridion is a concise Stoic manual that teaches how to achieve mental freedom and happiness by distinguishing between what is within our control (our thoughts, judgments, desires) and what is not (external events, other people's actions, our physical bodies). Through practicing proper use of impressions—accepting what we cannot change and focusing only on what we can change—we can achieve inner tranquility regardless of external circumstances.

---

## Book Summary

The Enchiridion is not a systematic treatise but rather a practical handbook of Stoic ethics, consisting of 53 brief chapters. Compiled by Arrian from his teacher's Discourses, it focuses almost exclusively on ethics, leaving the more abstract logical and physical teachings of Stoicism aside. The work opens with the foundational distinction: "Of things, some depend upon ourselves, others do not depend upon ourselves."

Epictetus argues that external events—wealth, health, reputation, death—are indifferent to true happiness because they lie beyond our control. What truly matters is our internal response to these events, specifically our judgments and desires. Freedom consists in wishing for nothing that is not up to ourselves and accepting everything that happens as fated by providence. The handbook provides concrete guidance on how to practice these principles in everyday life, covering relationships, grief, social roles, and the proper attitude toward death, poverty, and misfortune.

Throughout, Epictetus uses vivid analogies: life as a ship's voyage, a banquet, acting in a play, or staying at an inn. He emphasizes constant vigilance over one's reasoning, warning that it's not events that disturb people but their judgments about those events. The work concludes with memorable maxims for memorization and practice.

## Chapter-by-Chapter Summary

### Part One: What Is Up to Us (Chapters 1-21)

- **Chapter 1:** The fundamental distinction—some things depend on us (our judgments, desires, hates), others do not (body, possessions, reputation, external events)
- **Chapter 2:** Consequences of this distinction—those who realize what is not up to them will never blame gods or others for their misfortunes
- **Chapter 3:** Don't be disturbed by external things; if your neighbor's child dies, remember it is "nothing to you"
- **Chapter 4:** When going to the baths, consider what can go wrong; expect misfortune to happen
- **Chapter 5:** It is not things that disturb people but their judgments about things (famous opening to Chapter 5)
- **Chapter 6:** Death is nothing dreadful—if it were, Socrates would have feared it
- **Chapter 7:** Life is like a ship's voyage; we must play our assigned role well
- **Chapter 8:** Don't seek for things to happen as you wish; wish for them to happen as they do happen
- **Chapter 9:** Lameness is an impediment to the leg, not to the will—accept your circumstances
- **Chapter 10:** Don't be disturbed by the misdeeds of others; focus on your own actions
- **Chapter 11:** A child's death or loss of property is like leaving an inn—these things are not ours
- **Chapter 12:** How to prepare for difficult events: remember they are natural and inevitable
- **Chapter 13:** When you kiss your child, remember you may lose them—they are not yours to keep forever
- **Chapter 14:** Be content with whatever lot is given you
- **Chapter 15:** Life is like a banquet—take what is offered politely
- **Chapter 16:** When traveling, know the road; for life, follow reason
- **Chapter 17:** Life is like a play—the role assigned is what matters, not the role itself
- **Chapter 18:** Don't be angry with wrongdoers; they are simply mistaken about what is truly valuable
- **Chapter 19:** If anyone tells you that someone speaks ill of you, don't make excuses—just reply that he was unaware of your other faults
- **Chapter 20:** The key to tranquility is wanting whatever happens to happen
- **Chapter 21:** Death, exile, and everything thought terrible are indifferent—the only thing that matters is how we respond

### Part Two: Advice for Intermediate Students (Chapters 22-28)

- **Chapter 22:** If you have given up empty pretensions, you have gained true freedom
- **Chapter 23:** Remember you are an actor in a play of whatever length the director assigns
- **Chapter 24:** Progress means turning from things not up to us toward things that are
- **Chapter 25:** Don't discuss what a good man should be—be one
- **Chapter 26:** Ask yourself: "Is this something in my control?"
- **Chapter 27:** Shame is fear of losing good reputation; injustice is worse than death
- **Chapter 28:** What sort of person do you wish to appear to be?

### Part Three: Appropriate Actions (Chapters 30-47)

- **Chapter 30:** The proper function of life: to use things correctly according to reason
- **Chapter 31:** Three proper relationships: toward gods, toward others, toward ourselves
- **Chapter 32:** The essence of the good is choosing what is proper
- **Chapter 33:** Various moral precepts: don't be hasty, don't be distracted, show respect
- **Chapter 34-47:** Detailed guidance on justice, appropriate actions, and maintaining proper conduct in various situations

### Part Four: Practice of Precepts (Chapters 48-53)

- **Chapter 48:** Final advice on the types of people and how to deal with them
- **Chapter 49-52:** The practice and application of Stoic precepts
- **Chapter 53:** Memorable quotations for daily reflection and memorization

## Key Takeaways

1. **Distinguish what you can and cannot control.** Focus your energy only on what lies within your power—your thoughts, judgments, and choices. Everything else—external events, other people's opinions, your body—is beyond your control.

2. **It's not what happens, but how you judge it.** Events themselves are neutral; our suffering comes from our judgments about those events. Change your judgments, and you change your emotional state.

3. **Accept fate willingly.** Whatever happens is part of the natural order. Wishing for things to be different leads to frustration; accepting reality leads to peace.

4. **Practice constant vigilance.** Monitor your impressions and reactions continuously. Don't let automatic judgments run unchecked.

5. **Remember impermanence.** Everything external is temporary. Your children, your possessions, your life itself can be taken at any moment. This isn't pessimistic—it's liberating.

6. **Focus on virtue, not externals.** The only true good is living virtuously (wisdom, justice, courage, temperance). Wealth, health, and status are "preferred indifferents."

7. **Treat others with justice.** Because we share in rational nature, we owe respect and fairness to all human beings.

8. **Prepare for misfortune.** Expect difficulties. When they come, you'll be ready instead of surprised and devastated.

## Overall Assessment

The Enchiridion stands as one of the most accessible and practical works of ancient philosophy. Its enduring appeal lies in its directness and applicability—unlike abstract philosophical treatises, this manual offers wisdom you can use immediately. Epictetus's central insight remains as relevant today as it was two millennia ago: much of human suffering comes not from circumstances but from our mistaken beliefs about what we can and should control.

The work has proven its value over centuries. It influenced Christian monastic thought (through multiple Byzantine adaptations), shaped Renaissance thought, and was read by founding fathers like Jefferson and Franklin. Its popularity peaked in the 17th century during the Neostoicism movement, yet it remains a living text studied for personal development today.

The primary criticism of the Enchiridion is that it can seem overly deterministic or even fatalistic. The emphasis on accepting fate can be read as passive resignation. However, this misses Epictetus's point: acceptance of what we cannot change is not passivity but wisdom, freeing us to act effectively where we actually can make a difference—namely, in our own hearts and choices.

For modern readers seeking practical wisdom for navigating life's inevitable challenges—loss, disappointment, illness, conflict—the Enchiridion remains an invaluable guide. Its 53 brief chapters can be revisited throughout life, each reading revealing new depth in its simple but profound teachings.
