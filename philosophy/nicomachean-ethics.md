# Nicomachean Ethics

**Author:** Aristotle  
**First Published:** c. 350 BCE  
**Category:** Philosophy / Ethics  
**Genre:** Philosophical Treatise

---

## Author Background

Aristotle (384–322 BCE) was born in Stagira, a Greek city in Macedon, to a family of physicians. His father, Nicomachus, served as the personal physician to King Amyntas III of Macedon. After losing his parents at a young age, Aristotle was raised by a guardian and eventually became a student at Plato's Academy in Athens around 367 BCE, where he remained for nearly twenty years as both student and teacher.

Aristotle is often considered one of the greatest philosophers in Western history, credited with founding the Peripatetic school of philosophy. Unlike his teacher Plato, Aristotle's approach was more empirical and observational, emphasizing the study of the natural world and practical sciences. He tutored Alexander the Great during his years in Macedon and later returned to Athens to establish his own school, the Lyceum, where he wrote most of his known works.

## Publication Details

The Nicomachean Ethics was composed around 350 BCE during Aristotle's second period in Athens, likely during his time teaching at the Lyceum. The exact date of composition is uncertain, though a reference to a battle in the Third Sacred War in 353 BCE provides a terminus post quem. The traditional scholarly view holds that it was written during Aristotle's mature period (335-322 BCE).

The title "Nicomachean" is believed to refer to Aristotle's son Nicomachus, though some scholars suggest it may honor his father of the same name. The work consists of ten books (sections) and was likely based on lecture notes rather than a polished manuscript intended for publication. The text as we know it was preserved through a complicated transmission: after Aristotle's death, his works passed to Theophrastus, then through various hands and locations before being edited by Andronicus of Rhodes in the 1st century BCE in Rome.

The oldest surviving manuscript is the Codex Laurentianus LXXXI.11, dating to the 10th century CE. The work was first printed in Latin in the late 15th century and has been continuously studied and translated since.

## TLDR Summary

The Nicomachean Ethics is Aristotle's seminal work on moral philosophy, exploring how humans can achieve the highest good—eudaimonia, often translated as "happiness" or "flourishing." Aristotle argues that the good life involves living in accordance with virtue, developing excellent character traits through habit and practice, and using reason as our guide. The work examines key concepts including the golden mean (avoiding extremes), the importance of friendship, the nature of justice, and the role of intellectual and moral virtue in achieving a meaningful life.

## Book Summary

Aristotle's Nicomachean Ethics represents one of the foundational texts of Western moral philosophy, presenting a comprehensive examination of how human beings can achieve the good life. Unlike purely theoretical philosophical works, Aristotle explicitly states that his aim is practical—to help people become good and live well. The work is rooted in the Socratic tradition of examining how one should live, but approaches it characteristically through empirical observation and systematic analysis.

At the heart of Aristotle's ethical framework is the concept of eudaimonia, which he argues is the supreme good for humans. Unlike simple pleasure or momentary happiness, eudaimonia signifies a life of flourishing, engaged in excellent activity in accordance with virtue. Aristotle maintains that virtue is not innate but developed through habit and practice—we become courageous by performing courageous actions, just by performing just actions. This doctrine of the "golden mean" holds that moral virtues lie between extremes of excess and deficiency: courage, for instance, lies between rashness and cowardice.

The work also explores the role of reason in the good life, arguing that the distinctively human function is rational activity performed excellently. Aristotle examines various types of friendship—those of pleasure, utility, and virtuous character—and analyzes different forms of justice. The final books address the role of pleasure, the nature of intellectual virtue (particularly wisdom), and the relationship between ethics and politics, as Aristotle sees the political life as the highest form of human flourishing.

## Chapter-by-Chapter Summary

### Book I: The Chief Good
- Defines the good as that which all things aim at
- Establishes that there must be a supreme end (the highest good)
- Identifies eudaimonia (happiness/flourishing) as the supreme good
- Argues that political science is the master science concerning the good
- Discusses the importance of practical wisdom and experience

### Book II: Moral Virtue
- Introduces the concept of moral virtue as a habit (hexis)
- Presents the Doctrine of the Mean: virtue as the middle between excess and deficiency
- Explains that virtue is formed through practice and habit
- Discusses the relationship between voluntary action and moral responsibility

### Book III: Voluntary Action and Responsibility
- Examines the nature of voluntary and involuntary actions
- Discusses the role of choice (prohairesis) in moral action
- Analyzes the relationship between intention, forethought, and responsibility
- Explores the conditions for praise and blame

### Book IV: The Moral Virtues (Part I)
- Discusses courage as the mean between rashness and cowardice
- Examines temperance as the mean between insensibility and overindulgence
- Explores liberality (generosity) and prodigality/miserliness
- Addresses magnificence, pride, and good temper

### Book V: Justice
- Distinguishes between universal and particular justice
- Examines distributive justice (fair distribution of goods)
- Explains corrective justice (rectifying wrongs)
- Discusses equity and the interpretation of laws

### Book VI: Intellectual Virtue
- Introduces the five intellectual virtues: technē, phronēsis, epistēmē, sophia, and nous
- Emphasizes practical wisdom (phronēsis) as essential to moral virtue
- Explores the relationship between moral and intellectual virtue
- Discusses the role of judgment and moral perception

### Book VII: Continence and Incontinence
- Examines the nature of self-control and lack of self-control
- Explores the relationship between knowledge and action
- Discusses the role of pleasure and pain in moral life
- Addresses the problem of akrasia (weakness of will)

### Book VIII: Friendship (Part I)
- Defines the nature of friendship and its importance to the good life
- Distinguishes three types of friendship: of character, of pleasure, of utility
- Explores the qualities necessary for true friendship
- Examines the relationship between friendship and justice

### Book IX: Friendship (Part II)
- Continues examination of the various forms of friendship
- Discusses the role of friendship in political and social life
- Explores self-love and its relationship to friendship
- Examines the connection between friendship and happiness

### Book X: Pleasure and Happiness
- Analyzes the nature of pleasure and its role in the good life
- Critiques various philosophical theories of pleasure
- Argues that contemplative activity is the highest form of happiness
- Concludes with the relationship between ethics and politics

## Key Takeaways

1. **Eudaimonia is the highest good** — The goal of human life is not pleasure, wealth, or honor, but flourishing through excellent activity in accordance with virtue.

2. **Virtue is a habit** — Moral virtues are developed through repeated practice. We become good by doing good actions, not merely by thinking about them.

3. **The Golden Mean** — Excellence lies between extremes. Courage is the mean between rashness and cowardice; generosity is the mean between waste and stinginess.

4. **Practical wisdom (phronesis) is essential** — Knowing the right principle isn't enough; one must have the ability to apply that knowledge appropriately in particular situations.

5. **Friendship is crucial to human flourishing** — True friendships based on mutual respect and shared virtue are essential components of the good life.

6. **The function argument** — Humans have a unique function (reason), and living well means exercising that function excellently.

7. **Ethics and politics are connected** — The good life requires proper social and political institutions; ethics cannot be separated from political life.

8. **Contemplation is the highest activity** — While all virtues contribute to happiness, the contemplative life (theoretical wisdom) represents the highest form of human flourishing.

9. **Character and choice matter** — Moral development involves shaping our characters through habit while also making conscious choices about how to live.

10. **Happiness requires external goods** — While virtue is central, a complete life also requires certain external goods (health, wealth, friends) to flourish fully.

## Overall Assessment

The Nicomachean Ethics stands as one of the most influential and enduring works in Western philosophical tradition. Aristotle's approach to ethics—grounded in practical wisdom, the development of character through habit, and the pursuit of human flourishing—remains remarkably relevant today. His rejection of purely theoretical ethics in favor of a practical philosophy aimed at human improvement represents a watershed moment in moral thought.

The work's greatest strengths lie in its psychological realism, its integration of moral and intellectual virtues, and its recognition that ethics cannot be separated from human community and political life. Aristotle's doctrine of the mean provides a useful framework for thinking about moral balance, while his analysis of friendship and its role in the good life anticipates much modern psychological research on the importance of social bonds.

However, the work also faces significant criticisms. Some argue that Aristotle's conception of virtue is too tied to Greek city-state culture and may not translate easily to modern democratic societies. Others question whether his emphasis on habit and character adequately accounts for moral reasoning and principled action. The relationship between his ethical and political works raises questions about the role of institutions in moral development.

Despite these criticisms, the Nicomachean Ethics remains essential reading for anyone interested in moral philosophy, virtue ethics, or the foundations of Western thought. Its influence extends from medieval Scholasticism through contemporary virtue ethics, making it a cornerstone of philosophical education and a touchstone for ongoing debates about human flourishing, moral development, and the good life.
