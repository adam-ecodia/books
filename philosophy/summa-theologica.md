# Summa Theologica

**Author:** Thomas Aquinas  
**First Published:** Latin composition 1265–1274; English translation 1911  
**Category:** Philosophy / Medieval Theology / Scholasticism

---

## Author Background

Thomas Aquinas (1225–1274) was an Italian Dominican friar, theologian, and philosopher who stands as the most influential figure in medieval Scholasticism. Born into a noble family at Roccasecca, near Aquino, Italy, he was placed as an oblate at Monte Cassino monastery as a young boy. After encountering Aristotelian philosophy at the University of Naples, he chose to join the Dominican order, a decision his family initially opposed—they even had him abducted to prevent his journey to Paris to study.

Aquinas studied under the renowned Albertus Magnus at the University of Paris, where he earned his doctorate and later taught. He spent his career teaching at various universities, including Paris and Naples, and wrote extensively. His major works include the Summa Theologica, Summa contra Gentiles, and numerous commentaries on Aristotle. Aquinas was canonized in 1323 and declared a Doctor of the Church, recognized as the foremost Western philosopher and theologian of the Catholic Church. His doctrinal system is known as Thomism, which continues to influence Catholic theological education today.

## TLDR Summary

The Summa Theologica is a comprehensive systematic compendium of Christian theology and philosophy written by Thomas Aquinas between 1265 and 1274. It systematically addresses questions about God, creation, human nature, morality, Christ, and the sacraments using Aristotelian logic and scholastic method. Though left unfinished at Aquinas's death, it remains the most influential work in Western medieval philosophy and continues to shape Catholic theological education nearly 800 years later.

---

## Book Summary

The Summa Theologica represents the pinnacle of medieval scholastic thought, attempting to synthesize Christian theology with Aristotelian philosophy. Aquinas wrote this work primarily for theology students, intending to create an instructional guide that would present the reasoning behind Catholic doctrine in a systematic, accessible format.

The work is structured in three major parts. The **Prima Pars** (First Part) examines God's existence and nature, the creation of the world (including angels), and the fundamental nature of humanity. Aquinas addresses profound questions such as whether God's existence can be proven, the nature of divine attributes, and the relationship between God and creation.

The **Secunda Pars** (Second Part) is subdivided into two sections: the **Prima Secundae** deals with general moral principles, including the nature of happiness, virtue, law, and the fundamentals of human action; the **Secunda Secundae** examines specific virtues and vices in great detail—covering faith, hope, charity, justice, prudence, and fortitude, among others.

The **Tertia Pars** (Third Part) focuses on Christ and the sacraments, exploring the Incarnation, the nature of Christ's life and mission, and the seven sacraments of the Catholic Church. This section remains incomplete, as Aquinas died before finishing it. A posthumous supplement covers eschatology (the "last things": death, judgment, heaven, hell, and purgatory).

Throughout the Summa, Aquinas employs a distinctive method: each article presents several objections to a position, provides a counter-argument from authority, then offers his own reasoned response (respondeo dicendum), followed by replies to each objection. This format allows him to thoroughly examine every angle of complex theological questions.

- **Questions 27-32:** The Trinity—Father, Son, and Holy Spirit

- **Questions 65-74:** The work of the six days of creation
- **Questions 75-102:** Human nature—the soul, intellect, will, passions
- **Questions 103-119:** Providence, predestination, and the problem of evil

### Prima Secundae (First Part of the Second Part) — Questions 1-114

- **Questions 1-5:** The end of human life—happiness and beatitude
- **Questions 6-21:** Human acts—voluntary and involuntary actions, intention
- **Questions 22-48:** Passions of the soul—love, desire, hate, hope, fear, anger
- **Questions 49-70:** Habits and virtues—general theory of virtue
- **Questions 71-89:** The cardinal virtues—prudence, justice, fortitude, temperance
- **Questions 90-100:** Law—eternal law, natural law, human law, divine law
- **Questions 101-114:** Sin—nature, causes, and effects of sin

### Secunda Secundae (Second Part of the Second Part) — Questions 1-189

- **Questions 1-16:** Faith—acts of faith, the object of faith, heresy
- **Questions 17-22:** Hope—acts of hope, despair, presumption
- **Questions 23-46:** Charity—love of God and neighbor, the gifts of the Holy Spirit
- **Questions 47-58:** Prudence—right reason in action
- **Questions 59-88:** Justice—righteous dealing with others
- **Questions 89-100:** Fortitude—courage in the face of danger
- **Questions 101-126:** Temperance—moderation in pleasures
- **Questions 127-132:** Parts of justice—religion, piety, gratitude
- **Questions 133-170:** Vices opposed to virtues
- **Questions 171-189:** States of life—married life, virginity, religious life

### Tertia Pars (Third Part) — Questions 1-90

- **Questions 1-26:** Christ—Incarnation, personhood, knowledge
- **Questions 27-59:** Christ's life—his actions, suffering, death
- **Questions 60-90:** The sacraments—Baptism, Confirmation, Eucharist, Penance, Extreme Unction, Holy Orders, Matrimony

### Supplement — Questions 1-99

- **Questions 1-99:** Eschatology—resurrection, judgment, heaven, hell, purgatory, limbo

## Theological Analysis

### The Synthesis of Faith and Reason

Aquinas's greatest achievement in the Summa is his demonstration that faith and reason are harmonious, not contradictory. He insists that reason can demonstrate certain truths about God (such as His existence) while faith reveals mysteries that exceed reason (such as the Trinity). This approach, often called "Christian Aristotelianism," became the foundation for Western Catholic intellectual life.

### Natural Law Theory

The Summa articulates the classic formulation of natural law theory: eternal law (God's plan) is reflected in natural law (rational creatures' participation in eternal law), which then informs human law. This framework influenced Western legal philosophy, human rights discourse, and remains influential in contemporary Catholic social teaching.

### The Five Ways

In Question 2, Article 3, Aquinas presents his famous "Five Ways" to prove God's existence:
1. **Unmoved Mover** — Everything that moves is moved by something else; this chain must terminate in a first mover.
2. **First Cause** — Every effect has a cause; the chain of causes must begin with a first cause.
3. **Contingent Beings** — Things exist that could not exist; there must be a necessary being whose necessity is not derived.
4. **Degrees of Perfection** — Things possess varying degrees of good, true, noble qualities; there must be a maximum standard.
5. **Intelligent Design** — Objects lacking intelligence act toward ends; there must be an intelligent being directing them.

### Moral Philosophy

Aquinas's ethical framework integrates Aristotelian virtue ethics with Christian theology. Happiness (beatitude) consists in the vision of God, the ultimate good. Virtues are habits that perfect the soul's faculties, enabling humans to act in accordance with reason and divine order. The theological virtues (faith, hope, charity) direct humans to God, while moral virtues (prudence, justice, fortitude, temperance) order human life properly.

### Christology and Soteriology

The Third Part presents a thorough theology of Christ as the God-man who bridges humanity and divinity. Aquinas explores how Christ's suffering and death accomplish redemption, drawing on both biblical themes and Aristotelian concepts of causality and substitution.

## Key Takeaways

1. **Faith and Reason Are Complementary** — Aquinas demonstrates that rational inquiry supports and illuminates religious belief, not threatens it. This remains a foundational principle of Catholic intellectual tradition.

2. **The Existence of God Can Be Reasonably Defended** — The Five Ways offer classical arguments that continue to be discussed, critiqued, and defended in philosophical debates today.

3. **Natural Law Provides an Objective Moral Foundation** — Aquinas's framework grounds moral truth in rational nature and divine order, offering an alternative to relativist ethics.

4. **Virtue Requires Habit and Practice** — Moral excellence develops through repeated virtuous actions, forming character over time.

5. **Human Happiness Lies in Union with God** — True fulfillment comes not from worldly success but from the beatific vision—eternal communion with the divine.

6. **Systematic Thinking Reveals Order** — Aquinas's method shows how complex theological questions can be analyzed systematically, with attention to objections and counter-arguments.

7. **The Incarnation Resolves the Human Condition** — Christ, as fully divine and fully human, provides the path by which humanity can be restored to relationship with God.

## Overall Assessment

The Summa Theologica stands as one of the greatest intellectual achievements in Western civilization. Aquinas's ability to synthesize the philosophical framework of Aristotle with Christian theological tradition created a systematic approach to understanding God, humanity, and the moral life that has influenced thinkers for nearly eight centuries.

**Strengths:**
- Exceptionally clear and methodical structure
- Rigorous logical analysis balanced with spiritual depth
- Comprehensive coverage of theological topics
- Timeless insights into human nature and morality
- Model of how to engage opposing viewpoints fairly

**Challenges:**
- Dense, technical language can be inaccessible to modern readers
- Some conclusions reflect medieval assumptions no longer universally held
- The unfinished nature of the Third Part leaves certain topics incomplete
- Certain arguments (such as on the eternity of the world or usury) are historically situated

**Legacy:**
The Summa remains the foundational text of Thomistic philosophy and Catholic theological education. It influenced Dante (who called it "the Summa in verse" through his Divine Comedy), shaped Catholic social teaching, and continues to be studied in universities worldwide. Even for non-Catholic readers, it offers a masterclass in rigorous philosophical and theological reasoning.

For contemporary readers, the Summa rewards patience and careful study. Its insights into natural law, virtue ethics, and the relationship between faith and reason remain relevant to modern philosophical and ethical debates.
